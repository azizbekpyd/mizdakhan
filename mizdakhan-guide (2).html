Introduction

Welcome to Mizdakhan

Mizdakhan is an ancient necropolis — a huge, old cemetery — in Karakalpakstan, in western Uzbekistan. It sits on three low hills, about 20 km west of the city of Nukus, close to the town of Khojeli. People used this site for burials for about 1,700 years, from around the 4th century BC to the 14th century AD.

Tourists visit Mizdakhan because it feels different from any other place. It is not one single building. It is a whole hillside covered with mausoleums, old graves, a ruined fortress, and famous legends. The most photographed spot is the underground mausoleum of Mazlumkhan Sulu. Many visitors also love the 'World Clock' legend at Khalifa Erejep and the mysterious 25-metre tomb at Shamun Nabi.

Mizdakhan is not currently listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site on its own. However, it lies within the wider Khorezm region, whose ancient desert monuments are part of Uzbekistan's UNESCO Tentative List. This means the country hopes such sites may gain World Heritage status in the future.

Before visiting, tourists should know a few practical things. There is no entry fee, but many visitors leave a small donation. Modest clothing is expected, since this is a living, sacred site for local people, not only a museum. There are many stairs and uneven paths, so comfortable shoes are important. There are no toilets on site. Finally, guides should always remind tourists to be respectful — Mizdakhan is still an active place of prayer and pilgrimage for local families.

01

Mazlumkhan Sulu

(maz-LOOM-khan soo-LOO)
Location Eastern hill of Mizdakhan necropolis
Century 12th–14th century AD
Type Underground mausoleum
Period Medieval Khorezm / Golden Horde
Religion Islamic (built on an older holy place)
Interesting fact Most of the building is under the ground.

History

Mazlumkhan Sulu is the most famous building at Mizdakhan. People built it between the 12th and the 14th century. It has two parts. One part is above the ground. The other part is under the ground. Visitors walk down a small stone staircase into a large hall shaped like a cross. The hall has three domes above it.

Some historians think the underground room was first a place for prayer, not a grave. Later, people used it as a mausoleum. Inside, the walls have blue tiles with beautiful patterns. It is always cool inside, even in the hot summer.

The building is very old, but it is in good condition. This is because most of it is under the ground. The ground protected it from wind, sun, and war. Today, it is the number one stop for every visitor to Mizdakhan.

Legend (this is a local story, not a proven fact)

A long time ago, a ruler had a beautiful daughter named Mazlumkhan. Many rich men wanted to marry her. But she loved a poor architect. Her father was angry. He said: "Build a tower as tall as the sky in one night, and you can marry my daughter."

The architect worked all night. He finished the tower! But the ruler still said no. In great sadness, the architect jumped from the top of the tower. Mazlumkhan loved him so much that she jumped too.

The ruler was full of regret. He ordered his men to bury the two lovers together. He used the bricks of the broken tower to build their tomb. This is why local people call this place Mazlumkhan Sulu.

Architecture

Builders used baked bricks for this mausoleum. The plan of the underground hall looks like a cross. Three domes cover the hall from above. Small windows in the domes let soft light come down into the room.

The walls have blue and white tiles. This decoration is called majolica. The corners of the room have special carved shapes called muqarnas. They look like small stone honeycombs. There is also a small staircase that connects the ground level to the underground hall.

Why Tourists Like It

  • It is the most beautiful building in Mizdakhan.
  • The underground hall feels mysterious and cool.
  • The love story is romantic and easy to remember.
  • The blue tiles make great photos.
  • It is a short, easy walk from the main gate.
Guide Story ~1 minute

"Welcome to Mazlumkhan Sulu. Look down — most of this building is under our feet! Let's go down the stairs together. Watch your step, please.

Here, under the ground, is a large room with three domes. Local people say a princess named Mazlumkhan is buried here, next to the man she loved. Her father did not let them marry. So they both died for love, and her father built this tomb for them together.

Look at the blue tiles on the walls. This pattern is called majolica. It is many hundreds of years old, but it still looks beautiful. Take a photo here — this is one of the best spots in Mizdakhan."

Tourist Questions

Is the love story true?

No one knows for sure. It is a local legend, not a proven historical fact.

Why is the building underground?

Historians think it helped protect the building for hundreds of years.

How old is it exactly?

Experts believe it was built between the 12th and 14th centuries.

Can we go inside?

Yes. There is a small staircase. Please watch your step.

Who is buried here?

There are old graves inside, but the names on them are not clear today.

02

Shamun Nabi

(shah-MOON nah-BEE)
Location Northern hill of Mizdakhan necropolis
Century First built around the 13th–14th century; rebuilt later
Type Above-ground mausoleum with seven domes
Period Islamic period
Religion Islamic; the man in the legend preached one God
Interesting fact The tomb inside is over 25 metres long!

History

The Shamun Nabi mausoleum stands on the north hill of Mizdakhan. Builders made it from baked brick. It has seven domes. Seven domes are very rare in this region, so this building is special.

Inside, there is a very long tomb. It is more than 25 metres long! In 1966, archaeologists opened the tomb to look inside. They found no human bones at all. The tomb was empty.

The old building had many problems with water damage over the years. In 2018, workers took down the old structure. A new building was made to replace it. So the domes you see today are new, but they stand on a very old and holy place.

Legend (this is a local story, not a proven fact)

Local people say a holy man named Shamun Nabi came to this land long before the Prophet Muhammad. He could talk to animals. He could control the wind and the stars. He healed sick people.

A rival leader, called Giaur, lived nearby. Giaur was in love with Shamun's wife. One day, Shamun found them together. A fight started. Shamun's own loyal dog helped him and attacked Giaur.

There are many endings to this story. In one version, Shamun died from his wounds. People say his tomb grows longer every year, just like the famous tomb of Daniel in Samarkand.

Architecture

This mausoleum has a tall entrance and seven domes in a row. This design is unusual for the region — most buildings here have only one or three domes. Builders used baked bricks. Inside, one very long tomb takes up most of the space.

The current building is a modern reconstruction. The old original walls were weak and could not be saved. But the layout still follows the old, traditional shape.

Why Tourists Like It

  • The seven domes look impressive and unusual.
  • The 25-metre tomb is one of the strangest sights in Uzbekistan.
  • The legend about the loyal dog is a great story to tell.
  • It connects to other 'growing tomb' legends in Central Asia.
Guide Story ~1 minute

"Now we are at Shamun Nabi. Count the domes with me — one, two, three... seven! Seven domes in one line. This is very rare here.

Inside is a tomb. It is over 25 metres long. That is longer than a swimming pool! Local people believe a holy man named Shamun Nabi is buried here. They also say the tomb grows a little every year.

But here is a strange fact: in 1966, scientists opened the tomb. They found nothing inside. It was empty! So the mystery continues. Maybe you can solve it."

Tourist Questions

Is the tomb really growing?

This is a legend. Archaeologists say the tomb has not changed size.

Why was it empty inside?

No one knows for sure. This is one of the mysteries of Mizdakhan.

Is this the original building?

No. The old building was rebuilt in 2018 because it was damaged.

Who was Shamun Nabi?

Local legend says he was a holy man with special powers.

Why does it have seven domes?

Seven is a special number in local tradition. It also appears in other legends here.

03

Jomart Qassab

(jo-MART kas-SAHB)
Location Next to the Shamun Nabi mausoleum
Century Ancient; exact date unknown
Type Burial mound (a hill-shaped tomb)
Period Possibly linked to the Zoroastrian period
Religion Pre-Islamic legend, now a local Islamic holy place
Interesting fact The name means 'Jomart the butcher'.

History

Jomart Qassab is not a building. It is a mound — a small round hill about 5 metres high. It stands close to the Shamun Nabi mausoleum. Older, hidden structures may be underneath it, but they have not been fully studied.

Some researchers connect this mound to very old Zoroastrian beliefs. Zoroastrian people lived in this area long before Islam came. One idea says this mound may relate to Gayomard, the 'first man' in old Zoroastrian stories. Other people connect this place with the Islamic story of Adam.

Today, it is a small but meaningful stop on the Mizdakhan tour. It is quiet, unusual, and full of local belief.

Legend (this is a local story, not a proven fact)

Long ago, there was a butcher named Jomart. In years of famine, when food was hard to find, he gave meat to poor people for free. He asked for nothing in return.

People loved him for his kindness. After he died, they saw him as a saint. They believe the mound is built over his grave.

There is another local custom too: some people believe that a woman who wants a baby should roll down the mound seven times. This is a folk belief, and it is not related to modern medicine.

Architecture

This site is very simple. It is a round mound of earth, not a decorated building. There is no dome, no tile, and no carved stone. Its power comes from the story, not from architecture.

Because the mound is old and made of soft earth, walking on it can slowly damage it. Visitors should be careful and respectful.

Why Tourists Like It

  • It has a heart-warming story about kindness and generosity.
  • It shows a very different, simple side of Mizdakhan.
  • It connects visitors to very old, pre-Islamic beliefs.
  • It is a quick and easy stop next to Shamun Nabi.
Guide Story ~1 minute

"This small hill in front of us is Jomart Qassab. It does not look like much — just a mound of earth. But the story behind it is beautiful.

Local people say a butcher named Jomart lived here long ago. In hard years, when people were hungry, he gave meat to the poor for free. He never asked for money. After he died, people remembered his kindness, and they began to call this place holy.

So, this little hill is really a monument to generosity. Sometimes the smallest places have the biggest stories."

Tourist Questions

Is there a real grave inside?

Historians are not completely sure. Older structures may lie underneath.

Why is it shaped like a hill?

This is a traditional mound-style burial, common in ancient Central Asia.

Is the butcher story true?

It is a legend passed down by local people, not a documented historical fact.

Can we climb the mound?

It is best to walk carefully and follow your guide's advice to protect the site.

Why is it next to Shamun Nabi?

Both sites are part of the same sacred hill and share old local traditions.

04

Khalifa Erejep

(kha-LEE-fah eh-reh-JEP)
Location About 120 metres from Mazlumkhan Sulu
Century 9th–10th century AD
Type Ruined rectangular mausoleum
Period Early Islamic period
Religion Islamic
Interesting fact People call it the 'World Clock' of Mizdakhan.

History

Khalifa Erejep is one of the oldest religious buildings at Mizdakhan. Builders made it in the 9th or 10th century. It is a tall, rectangular structure. Today, one wall and part of the roof have fallen down.

Archaeologists studied the foundation of the building. They found a layer of reeds under the bricks. This layer helped protect the building from underground water. It also gave the structure some strength during earthquakes. This is why the building has survived so many centuries, even in ruins.

Today, visitors from many countries come here. Some come for religious reasons. Some come simply because the 'World Clock' legend is so interesting.

Legend (this is a local story, not a proven fact)

People say Khalifa Erejep taught his people for many years during his life. After he died, they believe his spirit continued to teach.

The most famous legend is the 'World Clock.' People say one brick falls from this building every year. When the very last brick falls, the world will end.

Because of this belief, visitors collect fallen bricks. They stack them into small towers of exactly seven bricks. Seven is a lucky number in local tradition. People believe this act helps to protect the world a little longer.

Architecture

The building has a simple rectangular shape, made from sun-dried and baked brick. Today, only three walls and parts of the roof remain. The foundation contains a hidden layer of reeds, which is an early form of earthquake protection.

There is little decoration compared to Mazlumkhan Sulu. Its power is in its age and its legend, not in fancy tiles or carvings.

Why Tourists Like It

  • The 'World Clock' legend is fun and easy to remember.
  • Tourists enjoy stacking seven bricks themselves.
  • It is one of the oldest structures in Mizdakhan.
  • The ruined look feels dramatic and photogenic.
Guide Story ~1 minute

"This ruin is called Khalifa Erejep. Local people call it the 'World Clock.' Look closely — do you see the small towers of bricks around us?

The legend says one brick falls from this old wall every year. When the last brick falls, it will be the end of the world! So visitors collect the fallen bricks. They build small towers with exactly seven bricks, because seven is a lucky number here.

Would you like to build one too? Please only use loose bricks from the ground, not bricks from the wall."

Tourist Questions

Will the world really end?

This is only a local legend. It is a story, not a scientific fact.

How old is this building?

It was built about 1,100 to 1,200 years ago, in the 9th or 10th century.

Why did it fall down?

Time, weather, and old age caused parts of the wall and roof to collapse.

Why do people stack seven bricks?

Seven is considered a lucky and complete number in local tradition.

Who was Khalifa Erejep?

Legend says he was a wise teacher who spread Islam in this region.

05

Mademin Khan

(mah-deh-MEEN khan)
Location Within the Mizdakhan necropolis, near other mausoleums
Century Medieval period (exact date not well documented)
Type Mausoleum
Period Islamic period
Religion Islamic
Interesting fact Historians know less about this site than the others.

History

The mausoleum of Mademin Khan is one of the many burial monuments inside the Mizdakhan necropolis. It stands among the many mausoleums, mosques, and graves that cover the hills of this ancient site.

Historians are not completely sure about the exact century when it was built or full details of its history. It is less studied and less famous than Mazlumkhan Sulu or Shamun Nabi. But it is still an important part of the whole necropolis, which holds more than a hundred tombs from different centuries.

Guides can tell tourists that Mizdakhan has many mausoleums like this one. Not every building has a famous legend, and that is completely normal for such a large and old necropolis.

Legend (local information is limited for this site)

There is no widely recorded legend for Mademin Khan that is separate and well documented, unlike Mazlumkhan Sulu or Shamun Nabi.

Guides should be honest with tourists: not every mausoleum in Mizdakhan has a famous story. This mausoleum is a good example to show tourists that Mizdakhan is a real, working necropolis with many graves, not only a place of legends.

Architecture

Like other mausoleums here, it uses local baked brick. It sits among a large group of graves and monuments on the hill. Compared to Mazlumkhan Sulu, it has simpler architecture and less decoration.

Guides do not need deep architectural detail here. It is enough to point it out as part of the wider necropolis landscape.

Why Tourists Like It

  • It shows the true scale of the necropolis — hundreds of graves, not just famous ones.
  • It is a good stop to explain how a necropolis works.
  • It gives tourists a break from long stories, with a shorter stop.
Guide Story ~1 minute

"Look around you. Do you see how many mausoleums and graves are here? This one is called Mademin Khan.

Not every mausoleum in Mizdakhan has a famous legend, and that's okay. This place is a real cemetery with a very long history. Some tombs are famous. Some are quiet and simple, like this one. Together, they show us the full story of Mizdakhan — a place used for burial for over 1,700 years."

Tourist Questions

Is there a legend for this place?

There is no well-known legend recorded for this site.

Why is it less famous?

It is simply less studied than the more decorated mausoleums nearby.

Who was Mademin Khan?

Historians do not have full, confirmed details about this person.

Is it still worth visiting?

Yes — it helps tourists understand the full size of the necropolis.

Are there many places like this here?

Yes, Mizdakhan has more than one hundred tombs and mausoleums in total.

06

Gyaur Qala (Gawir Qala)

(GYAH-oor kah-LAH)
Location About 1 km from the main Mizdakhan complex
Century 4th–3rd century BC
Type Ancient clay fortress
Period Pre-Islamic / early antiquity
Religion Zoroastrian (originally)
Interesting fact Its walls are up to 10 metres high.

History

Gyaur Qala is a large, ancient fortress. It stands on a hill about 1 kilometre from the main Mizdakhan hill, near the town of Khojeli. Builders made it around the 4th or 3rd century BC, long before Islam came to this region.

The fortress covers a huge area — about 200 hectares. Its walls are up to 10 metres high in places. Inside the walls, archaeologists found the remains of two castles from different time periods. The older castle still has some standing towers.

Experts believe the towers were not only for defence. Some may have also been used for religious ceremonies. Excavations found painted walls and carved clay decoration inside one of the castle courtyards. Gyaur Qala protected the people who once lived on the neighbouring hill, where the city of Mizdakhan later grew.

Historical background (based on archaeology, not legend)

Unlike some other sites at Mizdakhan, Gyaur Qala is mostly explained through archaeology, not folk stories. Its name comes from a word meaning 'infidel' or 'non-believer,' given during the period when Islam spread through the region.

In the legend of Shamun Nabi, a rival ruler called 'Giaur' is said to have lived in a castle here. Local storytellers connect this character to the name of the fortress, linking the two sites together in the region's oral history.

Architecture

Builders made Gyaur Qala from clay, using local building methods of the ancient period. Its outer walls are tall — up to 10 metres — and follow the natural edge of the steep hill. Inside, visitors can see a maze-like layout of two castles from different centuries.

One large courtyard once had living areas and ceremonial rooms around it. Traces of wall paintings and carved clay can still be found. A broken stone arch near the necropolis side marks where the old town gate once stood.

Why Tourists Like It

  • It shows a much older layer of history than the mausoleums.
  • The tall clay walls are impressive and dramatic.
  • It is quiet and far less crowded than the main necropolis.
  • It connects directly to the Shamun Nabi legend.
Guide Story ~1 minute

"Now we leave the necropolis and walk toward Gyaur Qala. This fortress is much older than the mausoleums we just saw — it was built over 2,000 years ago!

Look at these clay walls. Some are still 10 metres tall. This was once a strong, important fortress protecting the ancient city nearby. Remember the story about Shamun Nabi and his rival, Giaur? Local storytellers say this was Giaur's home.

Watch your step here — the ground has old, hidden rooms below, and some areas are unstable."

Tourist Questions

How old is Gyaur Qala?

It was built around the 4th or 3rd century BC, over 2,000 years ago.

What does the name mean?

It comes from a word meaning 'infidel' or 'non-believer.'

Is it connected to Shamun Nabi?

Yes, local legend links this fortress to the rival character Giaur in that story.

Is it safe to walk around?

Mostly yes, but visitors should be careful of unstable, crumbling ground.

Why is it less visited than the necropolis?

It is farther away and less known, so fewer tour groups stop here.

Chronology

Timeline of Mizdakhan

4th century BC
The first settlement is founded on the western hill. This is the start of Mizdakhan and the nearby fortress of Gyaur Qala.
4th century BC – 4th century AD
The city grows under Zoroastrian traditions. People bury their dead using ossuaries, special containers for bones.
5th – 8th century AD
The city keeps growing. New public buildings appear on the site.
7th – 8th century AD
Arab conquerors bring Islam to the region. Burial customs slowly change from Zoroastrian to Islamic practice.
9th – 10th century
Builders construct the Khalifa Erejep mausoleum, one of the oldest structures still visible today.
9th – 12th century
The city enters a period of revival. A fortified citadel is built.
12th – 14th century
Mizdakhan reaches its golden age under the Golden Horde. Builders construct Mazlumkhan Sulu and other grand mausoleums.
1221 AD
Genghis Khan's army destroys Mizdakhan and nearby Kunya Urgench during the Mongol invasion.
1388 AD
Amir Timur (Tamerlane) attacks the region again during his campaign toward Kunya Urgench.
14th century onward
The city is no longer a living town, but people continue using it as a sacred burial ground.
18th century
A later version of the Shamun Nabi mausoleum is built over the older ruins.
20th century
Local people continue building small tombs and mosques on the site, showing its long-lasting spiritual importance.
2018
The old Shamun Nabi structure is taken down due to water damage, and a new building is constructed in its place.
Today
Mizdakhan is a major tourist site in Karakalpakstan and remains an active place of prayer and pilgrimage.
